Where It Is At Today

With how far VR technology has advanced, it is hardly a surprise that it is being utilized in several different fields already. Perhaps the most commonly known application VR has to its name is its role in the video game industry. Due to its ability to surround the user in a simulated environment, VR gaming has a niche as arguably the most immersive and interactive experiences possible. With improving graphics, controls, and higher resolution than ever, (especially with the introduction of Meta Quest 3) VR technology continues to have a solid market here.

Another common use VR technology has is the ability to shop virtually. By simply wearing the headset, users can visit commercial stores, explore the interiors, and purchase products at their leisure, all from the comfort of their homes. VR headsets even possess the feature of allowing users to virtually try on outfits before purchasing. That specific idea has been replicated for the automotive industry as well, allowing users to test drive cars before buying.

While VR technology has shown to be greatly beneficial for preparing users for real world experiences (even within the realm of healthcare!) and providing the chance to travel to distant places instantly, it is not without its downsides. The most prominent of these downsides is the current lack of interest in the field. As there is no standard baseline for the development of VR software, it can be incredibly costly to create specialized equipment, and without a consistent audience to sell to, this means that VR prices remain consistently high. This in turn can lead to turning away even more of the general audience. Other flaws include the bulkiness that comes with the hardware and occasional connection issues in the software, but these are all issues that are being addressed going forward.
