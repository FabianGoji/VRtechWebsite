Where It Started

The original idea of VR technology is most commonly attributed to the mind of Stanley Weinbaum, who published the sci-fi short story entitled “Pygmalion’s Spectacles”, a tale of a boy who received a pair of goggles that places him a stimulating world made of holographic recordings. Released in 1935, this story is credited with introducing the idea of VR to the public consciousness and served as a prediction for what future technologies might achieve.

However, though it was Weinbaum’s work that popularized the idea, the basis for VR technology appears much earlier, almost a century earlier, in fact! In 1838, Sir Charles Wheatstone discovered stereopsis, the main component of depth perception used in binocular vision. His research demonstrated how both of our eyes obtain slightly different images that the brain combines into one, creating the illusion of depth within flat or even separate pictures. Sir Wheatstone’s stereoscope served as the prototype for this concept, and as a foundation for VR technology.

After the release of “Pygmalion’s Spectacles” in 1935, cinematographer Morton Heilig became the first to follow up on the idea, producing the first patented VR machine in 1962, called Sensorama. Functioning as a booth that could contain up to four occupants at a time, this invention used 3D video, audio, smells, vibrations, and more to stimulate all five of the audience’s senses. While six short films were successfully developed for it, it ultimately failed due to the lack of funds necessary to keep producing 3D films. While the Sensorama was not the ‘cinema of the future’ that Heilig was hoping for, it did pave the way for further improvements to be made.
