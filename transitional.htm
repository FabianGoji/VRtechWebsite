How It Transitioned

With a baseline established, the potential for VR became more widely recognized, and more improvements were being made every other year.

Following the decline of the Sensorama, Heilig returned with another attempt in 1960, patenting the Telesphere Mask as the first head-mounted display (HMD). While this model came with stereoscopic 3D wide vision and stereo sound, motion-tracking was not available in headset form until the introduction of Headsight a year later. Created by Bryan and Comeau of the Philco Corporation, Headsight additionally featured built-in screens for the individual eyes and a head-tracking system that utilized a remote camera to track motion, though it was used more for military use over VR ventures.

The next big step for VR came in 1965, when computer scientist Ivan Sutherland published what he called the Ultimate Display. The premise of his Ultimate Display was to create a headset capable of replicating a reality indistinguishable from our own. This would be achieved not only by utilizing 3D sound and tactile sensations in real time, but also giving users the ability to interact with the virtual world as realistically as possible. In this way, the Ultimate Display became the blueprint for most following VR inventions.

With the Ultimate Display in circulation, inventors were eager to prove themselves. The most critical of these was perhaps The Sword of Damocles in 1968, created by Sutherland and his student Bob Sproull. This giant, ceiling-hanging contraption was the first to implement a computer rather than a camera, requiring the user to be strap in before use. While it was too heavy to reasonably mass produce, it was able to produce wire frame shapes with ease, a novelty at the time.

From there, Myron Krueger set the basis for VIDEOPLACE technology with a series of “artificial reality” experiences. Starting in 1969, these projects were developed primarily using video systems. This cumulated with the unveiling of Krueger’s VIDEOPLACE at the Milwaukee Art Center in 1975. As the first interactive VR platform, it used a variety of tech such as projectors, graphics, video displays and cameras to surround the user and generate silhouettes that copied their movements, even allowing these silhouettes to interact with one another virtually. VIDEOPLACE was unique in that it didn’t require any gloves or goggles.

Created using snapshots from a car driving in the city, Aspen Movie Map allowed users to explore Aspen City virtually. Made by MIT in 1977, it also used no headset and instill the dream of using VR to view far away locations. McDonnell-Douglas Corporation integrated this idea for military use with their VITAL Helmet in 1979. Instead of a camera lens, the VR software would track the pilot’s eye movements to generate images.

The creation of the first wired gloves is attributed to Sandin and Defanti in 1982, who dubbed their creations ‘sayre gloves’. It incorporated gesture recognition into the realm of VR by converting finger movements into electrical signals. Similar technology was then developed by VPL Research, Inc., who also became the first to sell VR goggles and gloves in 1985.

Entering the realm of video games, Nintendo stepped into the fray of VR with the Virtual Boy Console in 1995, becoming the first portable console with 3D graphics. It was no commercial success, however, as user voiced dissatisfaction with the lack of software, color, and issues with comfort. It was discontinued and replaced with the VFX1 Headgear by Forte a year later.

2014 was the year that VR technology starting really taking off, with Facebook’s purchase of Oculus VR for $2 billion dollars. Suddenly several major brands starting taking action to have a hand in the new market, with Google releasing the Cardboard (stereoscopic viewer for smartphones), Sony announcing Project Morpheus (PS4 VR headset), and Samsung revealing the Samsung Gear VR (headset that directly worked in tandem with a Samsung Galaxy smartphone). From there, it was only a matter of time before more people jumped in…
